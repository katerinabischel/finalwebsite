ABSTRACT Remediating Ocean Acidification: Using Calcium Carbonate from Discarded Bivalves to Enhance Ocean Alkalinity by Katerina Bischel This thesis explores the use of discarded bivalve shells, primarily composed of calcium carbonate, to mitigate ocean acidification, a pressing threat to global marine ecosystems. The study suggests that reintroducing ground bivalve shells into marine environments can enhance water alkalinity, supporting the sustainability of organisms that rely on calcium carbonate structures. This research integrates traditional indigenous knowledge from the Bodega Bay community with contemporary scientific methods and includes a partnership with a local seafood restaurant to promote zero waste recycling. This collaboration provides a sustainable source of shells for the study and models an effective approach to waste management that benefits both the environment and academic research. Experimental trials conducted at the University of California, Santa Barbara REEF center focused on the effects of these shells on pH and other chemical parameters in ocean waters. The trials show that the addition of ground clam shells raised water pH levels from an initial average of 7.76 to 8.14, affirming their potential to counteract acidification. However, a decrease in dissolved oxygen levels was also observed, indicating a complex trade-off that requires further investigation. The study concludes with recommendations for future research to optimize shell dispersal methods to maximize environmental benefits while addressing potential drawbacks. iii iv
